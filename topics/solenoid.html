Exploring the behaviour and properties of magnets and their fields

What is a Magnet?

A magnet is an object that produces a magnetic field. This field exerts a force on other magnets and magnetic materials (like iron, nickel, and cobalt). Magnets have two poles: North (N) and South (S). Like poles repel, unlike poles attract.

Magnetic Fields Around a Bar Magnet

The magnetic field is the region around a magnet where magnetic forces can be detected. Field lines show the direction and strength of the field: they emerge from the North pole and enter the South pole.

Magnetic field lines emerge from North and enter South

Magnetic Field Around a Current-Carrying Wire

When an electric current flows through a wire, it creates a circular magnetic field around the wire. The direction of the field can be found using the right-hand rule: if you grip the wire with your right hand so your thumb points in the direction of the current, your fingers curl in the direction of the magnetic field.

Magnetic field forms concentric circles around the wire

Magnetic Field of a Solenoid

A solenoid is a coil of wire. When current flows through it, the magnetic fields from each loop combine to produce a strong, uniform field inside the coil, similar to a bar magnet. The field outside is weak and spread out.

Inside a solenoid, the field is strong and uniform

Summary

  • Magnets create magnetic fields, strongest at the poles
  • Magnetic field lines always go from North to South outside the magnet
  • Electric currents produce magnetic fields (Oersted's discovery)
  • Solenoids create strong, uniform magnetic fields similar to bar magnets
